The importance of colors

Colors play a crucial role in UI design, influencing user experience and conveying brand identity. Careful color selection can enhance usability, guide attention, and create a cohesive visual experience.

What do colors determine?

    Hierarchical

    Color indicates which elements are interactive, how they relate to other elements, and their level of prominence. Important elements should stand out the most.

    Legible

    Text and important elements, like icons, should meet legibility standards when appearing on colored backgrounds.

    Expressive

    Show brand colors at memorable moments that reinforce your brand’s style.

The 60-30-10 rule

In the design world, the 60-30-10 rule is a rule that helps to guide designers on choosing and pairing colors for their designs.

To put it simply, this rule says that the dominant/primary color should take up 60% of your design, the secondary color should take up 30%, while the accent color should take up 10% of your design.

How to choose colors for your designs

The 60-30-10 rule has three components:

Primary Color 60
Secondary Color 30
Accent Color 10

This is the dominant color of the design. It forms the foundation of the color scheme and covers the majority of the design (60%). The primary color is typically a neutral color and is often used as the background of the design. Colors like white, blue, beige, and so on can be used as primary colors.

This color supports the dominant color and adds more visual interest to the design. It covers about 30% of the design. The secondary color can be used for typography, icons, and subheadings/subtitles in a design. Colors like teal, black, dark blue, and others can be used as secondary colors.

This color usually covers about 10% of the design. It helps highlights specific sections of the design like buttons, call-to-action elements, or any element that needs emphasis in a design. Accent colors are usually colors that have high contrast. Colors like yellow, orange, light green, and others make good accent colors.

The Illusions

Lilac Chaser

As your gaze lingers, a fleeting green disc seems to chase them across the gray backdrop. A dynamic interplay of perception and afterimage, this illusion challenges the limits of vision. A testament to the brain’s intricate dance with visual stimuli, it’s an illusion that leaves an indelible mark.

Ebbinghaus Illusion

The Ebbinghaus illusion stands as a captivating example of perception’s malleability. Named after its discoverer, Hermann Ebbinghaus, this illusion highlights the brain’s tendency to compare and contrast visual elements, often leading to surprising discrepancies in size perception.
